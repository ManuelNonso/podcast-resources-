Part One

Discover

Surface what you have been carrying. Name the pattern. Give it language. This is the part of a coaching session where the thing you have not been able to say out loud finally gets said.

1
Before You Begin

A note on how to do this work

Every question in this worksheet is designed to be answered honestly, not cleverly. The polished answer is the one you already know. It has not changed anything. What we are going for here is the honest one — the one that sits a little lower in your chest, the one that takes a breath before it comes out. That is the one that does the work.

1
Name the Thing
Surface Awareness — Layer One

In the episode, the coach stayed with a broken tool because leaving felt worse than the reality of being stuck. The situation you are holding on to might be a tool, a habit, a relationship, a role, a belief, a version of your business, or a version of yourself. The form does not matter. What matters is the honesty.

Coaching Question

Right now, in your life — what is the thing you are staying in, holding on to, or continuing to use, that you already know is not working?

Not what you suspect might not be working. What you already know. Name it specifically.

Your Answer
Why This Matters

The first thing that came to mind is the right answer. Your mind offered it before your defences arrived. Do not rationalise your way past it. Work with what surfaced first.

How Long Have You Known?

The Body Knows First
Surface Awareness — Reading the Physical Signal

Before the mind constructs a reason to stay, the body already has a response. The tightness in the chest when you imagine leaving. The flatness in your energy every time you return to it. The subtle relief that flickers when you let yourself imagine a different path. These are not weak signals. They are precise ones.

Coaching Question

When you imagine releasing this — fully, completely, no going back — what is the first physical sensation that comes up? Where do you feel it in your body?

Define

Trace the grip to its root. Understand where the two fears actually came from. Separate the inherited pattern from your actual identity and capability.

2
Root Identification — Layer Two

Where the grip actually came from

The resistance you feel toward the new is not coming from your present-day logic. Your logic already knows what needs to change. The resistance is coming from somewhere older — a moment, or a pattern of moments, where uncertainty felt genuinely dangerous. This part of the session is about tracing it.

4
The Origin of the Grip
Root Identification — Tracing the Fear to Its Source
Coaching Question

When did you first learn that releasing something before you had a guaranteed replacement was not safe?

Not in those words. When did you first absorb that message? It might have been a moment you experienced directly. It might be something you watched happen to someone else. It might be a story told in your family. Whatever comes up — write it.

The Original Moment or Message
The Key Distinction

What happened in that moment was real. The conclusion your system drew from it — that the unknown is always dangerous, that releasing the familiar always leads to loss — that conclusion was a reasonable response then. The question for this session is whether it is still an accurate response now.

What Decision Did You Make in That Moment?
5
What You Are Actually Protecting
Root Identification — The Identity Layer

Beneath the practical reasons to stay — the time invested, the risk of uncertainty, the possibility that the new thing might not be better — there is almost always an identity question. Who am I if I am not the person who stayed? Who am I if I release this? This is the layer most people never reach.

Coaching Question

What would walking away from this cost you in terms of how you see yourself — not practically, but in terms of your identity?

The Identity Cost
Who I Believe I Am If I Stay
Who I Am Afraid I Become If I Leave

6
Your Evidence File
Separation and Reorientation — Layer Three

The fear says the unknown is not worth the risk. But your own history is full of moments where you released something familiar and found something on the other side that was worth it. The fear does not have access to that evidence. This exercise is about collecting it.

Coaching Question

What evidence already exists in your own life — not what you hope might be true, but what is already documented in your own experience — that stepping into the unknown produced something worth it?

Evidence Entry 1 — A Time You Let Go and Found Something Better
Evidence Entry 2 — A Time You Started Again and It Was Worth It
Evidence Entry 3 — A Time Someone in Your Life Did This and You Watched What Happened
The Reframe

Your fear is making decisions based on one data set — the times things went wrong when you left the familiar. Your evidence file is the other data set. A well-functioning internal decision-maker uses both. The goal of this exercise is to make sure both data sets are present in the room when your next decision gets made.

"The fear tells you the risk is not worth it. Your own history tells a different story. Which one are you letting make decisions for you right now?"

Part Three

Pressure Test

The real work happens under pressure. These scenarios test whether what you have built in Parts One and Two holds when the familiar makes its case for staying.

3
Framework Under Pressure

Stress-testing your clarity

Insight produced in a quiet room is a starting point. What matters is whether that insight holds when your system is under pressure — when the familiar reasserts itself, when the stakes feel high, when the voice that says "stay" gets louder. These three scenarios are designed to test exactly that.

Scenario 01

The Comfortable Return

You have decided to release the familiar thing — whatever you named in Part One. You have taken one step in a new direction. And then, unexpectedly, the old situation becomes available again. It is familiar. It has worked before. The sunk cost is whispering that you should go back. The uncertainty of the new path makes the old one look better than it ever actually was.

In this moment, which version of you is making the decision — the version with access to your evidence file, or the version that is responding to discomfort?

What is the one question you would ask yourself to distinguish between genuine reconsideration and fear-driven retreat?

Your Response to This Scenario
Scenario 02

The First Stumble

You have released the familiar. You have started something new. And the first attempt does not go the way you hoped. The new thing did not immediately produce better results. The familiar is now sending you a message: "See? You should have stayed. This was a mistake." And part of you is listening.

How do you distinguish between "this new thing is genuinely not working" and "this new thing requires more time than I expected"?

What is the rule you are going to use to decide how long to give the new before you assess it fairly?

Your Decision-Making Rule for This Scenario
Scenario 03

The Accountability Moment

Three months from now, someone who knows you well asks you what changed after you did this work. You have two possible answers. In the first version, you took the step, and something shifted — not perfectly, but meaningfully. In the second version, you did this worksheet, felt the shift, and then returned to exactly where you were. You are living in one of these two futures right now.

What is the single decision — not a habit, not a system, one decision — that determines which version you are living in?

What would you need to put in place today so that three months from now, you can answer that question the way you want to?

The One Decision
