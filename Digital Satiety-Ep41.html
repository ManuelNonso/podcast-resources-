This worksheet is an extension of the coaching session in this week's episode. It is designed to be done in one sitting — deep, honest, and without interruption. Set aside 60 minutes. Close the tabs. Put the phone face-down. What follows is the real work.

Part 1
Discover — Where Has Your Creative Voice Gone?
These exercises surface the pattern without judgment. The goal is clarity, not criticism. Name what is true before you try to change anything.
1
The First Move Audit
Surface awareness — what actually happens in your first sixty seconds

Think of the last three times you sat down to create something from scratch — a strategy, a piece of content, a presentation, a proposal, a creative piece. For each one, identify your first move in the first sixty seconds.

What does the pattern in those first moves tell you about how much you currently trust what is already inside you?

Don't answer this in your head. Write it down. The written answer is almost always sharper than the thought.

📌 Coaching Note

The first move is almost always diagnostic. If it is external — a tab, a scroll, a reference file — that is not a workflow preference. It is a trust signal. It tells you something specific about the relationship between your conscious mind and your creative resource. Neither judgment nor shame is required here. Just precision.

2
The Pride Inventory
Root identification — tracing your best work to its source

Think of the last piece of work you produced that made you genuinely proud — not pleased, not relieved it was done, but proud in your body. The kind of proud that feels different.

How much of that work came directly from inside you — unmediated by anything you had consumed first? Be honest. Not what you wish was true. What was actually true?

When did you last produce work that felt genuinely, distinctly yours — like it could only have come from you? How long ago was that?

If the answer is closer than you expected, that is worth noting. If it is further than you expected, that is also worth noting. Both are data.

3
Naming the Fear
Root identification — finding what the scrolling is actually managing

The scrolling and referencing before creative work is almost never really about research. It is about managing something. This exercise names it.

If you had to begin your most important creative project tomorrow — with no references, no research, no internet, no notes, just you and a blank page — what is the fear? Name it as specifically as you can. Not "I don't know what to say." What specifically are you afraid would happen or be revealed?

When did you first learn that this might be true about you? Not in those words — but when did you first absorb the message that your ideas alone might not be enough?

This might go further back than you expect. Let it.

What evidence in your own body of work directly contradicts that fear? Not what you hope is true. What is already demonstrably, verifiably true?

📌 Coaching Note

The fear that your thinking is not enough is almost always rooted in a specific formative experience — a time when original thinking was judged, compared, or dismissed. That experience is not evidence about your actual creative capacity. It is a data point from a specific moment in a specific context. The evidence in your current body of work tells a different story. That story is the accurate one.

4
The Inspiration Audit
Surface awareness — measuring your current consumption ratio

List the last ten pieces of content you consumed — articles, social posts, videos, podcasts, newsletters, books. For each one, mark whether it genuinely expanded your thinking (introduced something you had not considered before) or confirmed what you already believed.

Part 2
Define — Build Your Personal Creative Protection System
Using what you discovered in Part 1, build the framework that is specifically yours. Not a generic productivity system. A precise response to your specific pattern.

🔧 Your Personal Creative Protection Framework

Complete every field. Vague intentions do not survive the week. Specific commitments do.

Define exactly what "create before you consume" looks like in your daily rhythm. What time? What format? What is the minimum viable version of this that you will actually do?
Name the specific day and time. Name the first problem or project you will take into it. Block it now. Do not leave this exercise without a specific time in your calendar.
Based on your audit, name two non-negotiable consumption rules. What will you no longer do by default? What will you only do by intentional choice?
What is the most original piece of work you have been avoiding because you have been waiting to feel ready? Name it specifically. Then name the first thirty-minute block this week when you will begin it.
Part 3
Pressure Test — When the Framework Meets Reality
These scenarios test your framework against the specific moments when it will be most tempted to collapse. Work through each one before the week begins.

Scenario 1 — The High-Stakes Brief

You have a major pitch, strategy document, or creative brief due in 48 hours. Your first instinct is to spend the first two hours researching what others have done. Apply the Blank Page First Rule here. What specifically do you do instead?

Scenario 2 — The Flatness Day

You sit down to create and feel completely flat. The internal voice seems entirely absent. Everything you put on the page feels generic. You are tempted to open Instagram, LinkedIn, or Pinterest for "a quick look." What do you do instead?

Scenario 3 — The Comparison Spiral

You come across someone else's work that is — by every measure — extraordinary. Your own work suddenly feels inadequate by comparison. The instinct is to consume more of their content to understand what they are doing and incorporate it. What is the more useful response?

📌 Coaching Note

Extraordinary work in others is most useful as a signal that it is possible — not as a blueprint to replicate. The question that breaks the comparison spiral: "What does this person's work tell me about what is possible? Not about what I should do — about what can be done?" Then close the tab and return to your own page.

Final Reflection
"The work that is most yours — the work only you can make — is waiting on the other side of the discomfort. You do not need more inspiration. You need more hunger."
